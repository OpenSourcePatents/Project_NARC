# The Kingpin Fallacy

## Historical Evidence: Why Leadership Removal Does Not Reduce Drug Supply

---

## The Pattern

Every major cartel leadership removal in the last 30 years has failed to reduce drug supply. The pattern is consistent: removal triggers fragmentation and retaliatory violence without disrupting the underlying infrastructure.

| Year | Event | Result |
|---|---|---|
| 1993 | Pablo Escobar killed | Cali Cartel filled vacuum immediately. Drug trade continued. |
| 2000s | Tijuana Cartel dismantled | Sinaloa Cartel expanded to fill gap. |
| 2009 | Arturo Beltran Leyva killed | Organization fractured into smaller, more violent groups. |
| 2010 | Ignacio Coronel (Sinaloa) killed | Sinaloa operations continued. |
| 2014/2016 | El Chapo captured | Sinaloa operations continued uninterrupted under Zambada. |
| 2024 | El Mayo Zambada arrested/extradited | Sinaloa fractured into civil war. Fentanyl supply unchanged. Culiacan homicide rate increased 400%. |
| Feb 2026 | El Mencho killed | CJNG launched coordinated violence across 20 states. Retaliatory attacks within hours. Supply chain status: intact. |

## Why It Fails

The kingpin strategy assumes organizations depend on their leaders. Fentanyl supply chains do not.

- Synthesis labs are cheap, mobile, and replaceable.
- Leadership is replaceable (often by more violent successors).
- Precursor supply chains operate independently of any individual leader.
- Financial laundering networks (CMLOs) serve whoever is buying.
- Distribution networks at the retail level are decentralized.

The infrastructure survives every decapitation.

## The Fragmentation Effect

When the government launched its war on drugs in 2006 and prioritized kingpin targeting, cartel organizations splintered. According to UCDP data, the number of criminal organizations involved in at least one death rose from 2 in 2005 to 23 in 2023.

More organizations = more violence = less accountability = harder to target.

## The Alternative

This is why PROJECT NARC: TOTAL KILL CHAIN targets infrastructure, not individuals. Kill the money, kill the supply chain. The precursor pipeline, the financial laundering loop, the distribution networks, and the social media storefronts are all more durable than any individual leader -- but they are also more visible, more traceable, and more disruptable through data.
