# Platform Accountability Framework

## Proposed Obligations for Social Media Platforms

---

## The Problem

Social media is the primary marketplace for fentanyl dealers targeting young Americans. Dealers advertise using emoji codes, conduct transactions via encrypted DMs, accept payment through Cash App/Venmo/crypto, and deliver via mail or meetup.

- A CBS News investigation found a dealer within 48 hours using a fake 18-year-old student profile.
- Snapchat transactions have been linked to fentanyl deaths in at least 19 states.
- Instagram reported actioning 1.8 million pieces of drug content in 2022, yet dealers create new accounts within hours of bans.
- Platform moderation uses keyword matching. Dealers use emoji codes that evolve faster than algorithms.

Platform moderation has demonstrably failed to prevent dealer access to buyers.

## Proposed Obligations

### 1. Mandatory Reporting

Platforms must report suspected drug dealing activity to law enforcement, analogous to CSAM mandatory reporting under 18 U.S.C. 2258A.

Drug dealing on social media kills children. The reporting obligation should be identical to the one that already exists for child exploitation content.

### 2. Data Preservation

Upon law enforcement notification that a social media account is associated with a fatal overdose, platforms must preserve all account data, including "disappeared" messages, for a minimum of 180 days.

Current practice varies by platform and often results in critical evidence being automatically destroyed before law enforcement can obtain legal process.

### 3. API Access for Law Enforcement

Platforms should provide structured data access to authorized law enforcement via API, enabling the SOCIAL SCAN module to operate efficiently on public content rather than relying on manual scraping.

### 4. Accountability Metrics

Platforms must publish quarterly transparency reports including:
- Drug-related content detected and removed
- Dealer accounts banned
- Law enforcement referrals made
- Disaggregated by drug type
- Outcomes (linked deaths, arrests resulting from referrals)

## Legal Mechanisms

These obligations could be implemented via:
- Amendment to Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act (47 U.S.C. 230)
- FTC rulemaking under existing consumer protection authority
- New legislation modeled on the EARN IT Act framework adapted for drug trafficking
