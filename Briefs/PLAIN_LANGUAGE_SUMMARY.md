# PROJECT NARC: TOTAL KILL CHAIN -- Plain Language Summary

## What is this project?

Right now, when someone dies from fentanyl, here's what happens: a paramedic shows up, a medical examiner writes a report, a local cop files a case, and maybe -- maybe -- someone eventually figures out who sold the pill. That information sits in a folder in one office in one town. Nobody else ever sees it.

Meanwhile, the chemicals used to make that pill were advertised on a Chinese website, paid for with Bitcoin, shipped to Mexico labeled as dog food, pressed into a fake Percocet in a cartel lab, smuggled across the border in a truck full of legitimate goods, sold by a dealer on Snapchat using emoji codes, and paid for through Cash App. Every single one of those steps left a digital trail. But no one is connecting those trails into one picture.

That's what this project does.

## How does it work?

Think of it like this. Imagine you had a murder investigation where the killer left fingerprints at ten different crime scenes in ten different cities, but none of those police departments talk to each other. Each one thinks they have an unsolved case. If you put all ten fingerprint files in one room, you'd solve all ten in an afternoon. That's the situation with fentanyl right now, except it's not ten cases. It's 70,000 deaths a year.

The system has four parts.

### The Radar

Right now, nobody knows a town is in crisis until the bodies pile up and someone notices. This system tracks overdoses in real time, adjusts for population size, and automatically sends an alert when a community's overdose rate crosses into danger territory. Ten deaths in LA is normal. Ten deaths in a town of 3,000 is a five-alarm fire. The system knows the difference.

### Follow the Money

The chemicals to make fentanyl come from Chinese companies that literally advertise on the internet. They take Bitcoin. That Bitcoin is traceable. The cartels launder their drug cash through Chinese brokers who convert it to crypto, buy electronics, ship them to Mexico, and sell them for clean cash. Every step of that leaves financial fingerprints. This system tracks those fingerprints from the Chinese factory to the cartel bank account.

### Find the Dealers

Dealers sell fentanyl on Snapchat, Instagram, and TikTok using emoji codes. They take payment through Cash App and Venmo. They ship through the mail. They're not hiding. They're advertising. This system scans for those patterns, figures out who the dealer actually is, and builds a case file automatically. It also connects every overdose death back to the dealer who sold the fatal dose.

### Make Everyone Share

The biggest problem isn't technology. It's that the DEA, FBI, Border Patrol, IRS, state police, local cops, and medical examiners all have pieces of the puzzle and none of them share with each other. This system puts all the data in one place with different access levels -- a small-town cop sees what they need, a federal agent sees more, classified intelligence stays classified. And the key: if your agency doesn't contribute data to the system, you don't get counter-fentanyl funding. That's the only thing that makes government agencies cooperate.

## What does it cost?

The whole thing costs about $108-176 million a year. That sounds like a lot until you realize fentanyl costs the country somewhere between $280 billion and $770 billion a year in deaths, healthcare, and lost productivity. The system pays for itself if it prevents just 16 to 44 deaths out of 70,000. The money is already sitting in Congress's budget. It just needs somewhere to go.

## The bottom line

One system. Detect the crisis, trace the money, find the dealers, build the cases, measure whether it's working. Right now nobody does all of that. This does all of that.
